# Genre Playbook for Short-Form Viral Stories

Each genre has conventions the audience knows and expects. The challenge: fulfill those conventions in a way they have never seen. Keep convention, kill cliche.

## Horror

**Core value:** Safety → Danger / Life → Death
**Convention:** The threat must escalate beyond what the character (and audience) believed possible.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with normalcy hiding one wrong detail
- Escalate by removing safe exits one by one
- Turn by revealing the threat was closer/different than assumed

**Subgenres to play with:**
- **Uncanny:** Source of horror has rational explanation (stalker, technology, human evil)
- **Supernatural:** Source is irrational (ghosts, demons, curses)
- **Super-Uncanny:** Audience cannot tell which — maximize this ambiguity in short-form

**What works on TikTok:** Stories told from inside the danger. First person. Present tense. The narrator doesn't know what the audience suspects. The final line confirms the worst.

**Hook patterns:**
- The wrong detail in a safe place ("The baby monitor showed my daughter's room. She was waving at the corner. Nothing was there.")
- The rule that must not be broken ("My grandmother had one rule about the basement.")
- The pattern that changes ("Every night at 3am, my dog stares at the same wall. Last night, something stared back.")

## Romance

**Core value:** Love → Hate / Intimacy → Alienation
**Convention:** Two people who should be together face obstacles that keep them apart. They must meet, and meeting must feel inevitable yet fresh.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with connection or its painful absence
- Escalate through misunderstanding, bad timing, or impossible circumstances
- Turn by revealing what one character sacrificed/felt all along

**What works on TikTok:** Micro-romances that compress an entire love story into one scene. The unsaid matters more than the said. Subtext is everything.

**Hook patterns:**
- The missed connection ("I left my number on his coffee cup. He came back every day for a month. I'd already quit.")
- The sacrifice reveal ("She asked why I always order two coffees.")
- The impossible timing ("We matched on the app the day before his flight. One-way ticket.")

---

## Thriller / Suspense

**Core value:** Safety → Danger / Control → Helplessness
**Convention:** The threat must become personal. Stakes escalate from abstract to intimate.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with a ticking clock or discovery that changes everything
- Escalate by making each escape route a new trap
- Turn by revealing the threat was inside the trusted circle

**What works on TikTok:** Paranoia stories. The narrator discovers something that puts them in danger, and every attempt to get safe makes it worse. The audience knows before the narrator does.

**Hook patterns:**
- The discovery ("I found a camera behind my bathroom mirror. It wasn't mine.")
- The countdown ("My phone buzzed: 'You have 11 minutes.' No sender.")
- The wrong person ("The locksmith finished changing my locks. Then I saw his van was still parked outside at 2am.")

---

## Comedy

**Core value:** The overriding convention is: nobody gets hurt. Pain is present but never real.
**Convention:** Characters bounce through escalating absurdity. The audience must feel safe to laugh.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with a situation that is slightly off
- Escalate through misunderstanding compounding on misunderstanding
- Turn with the most absurd yet logical conclusion

**Subgenres:**
- **Romantic comedy:** The meet-cute gone wrong
- **Satire:** The system exposed through one ridiculous moment
- **Black comedy:** Cross the line — the audience laughs and feels guilty
- **Farce:** Escalating chaos from one small lie or mistake

**What works on TikTok:** Relatable absurdity. The audience sees themselves. Exaggerate real life by 20%, not 200%. Deadpan delivery of insane situations.

**Hook patterns:**
- The overreaction ("My wife asked me to 'pick up something nice for dinner.' I came home with a lobster tank.")
- The misunderstanding chain ("I told my mom I was seeing someone. She booked a church.")
- The accidental escalation ("I meant to text my boss 'I'll be late.' Autocorrect sent 'I'll be your fate.'")

## Drama / Social Drama

**Core value:** Justice → Injustice / Hope → Despair / Connection → Isolation
**Convention:** Identifies a problem in society or relationships, then constructs a story that reveals its human cost.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with a moment of injustice or emotional fracture
- Escalate by showing the cost of the situation on a specific person
- Turn by revealing a deeper truth about the system or relationship

**Subgenres for short-form:**
- **Domestic drama:** Family secrets, betrayals, sacrifices
- **Medical drama:** Life-or-death decisions, impossible choices
- **Psycho-drama:** Mental illness, identity, perception vs. reality

**What works on TikTok:** One human moment that stands for a larger truth. Specificity creates universality. A single scene that makes the audience feel something they recognize but have never articulated.

**Hook patterns:**
- The quiet devastation ("My father's last text was a grocery list. Milk, bread, eggs. He died on the way to the store.")
- The system failure ("The nurse told me my insurance didn't cover the treatment. My daughter was in the next room.")
- The invisible sacrifice ("My mom worked three jobs. I found out when I was 30.")

## Mystery

**Core value:** Knowledge → Ignorance / Truth → Deception
**Convention:** A crime or puzzle must exist. Clues must be planted. The audience races to solve it.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with an unanswered question that demands resolution
- Escalate by adding clues that reframe the mystery
- Turn by revealing the answer was hidden in plain sight

**What works on TikTok:** The audience loves to solve alongside the narrator. Plant two clues, withhold one, then deliver the answer that makes both clues click. First-person mysteries where the narrator is unreliable work well.

**Hook patterns:**
- The impossible detail ("My sister's diary had entries dated after her funeral.")
- The clue that doesn't fit ("Everyone in the photo was smiling. Except the person who took it.")
- The witness contradiction ("He said he was home all night. His fitness tracker said 14,000 steps.")

---

## Sci-Fi

**Core value:** Varies — often Knowledge → Ignorance, Freedom → Oppression, or Humanity → Dehumanization
**Convention:** One speculative premise, then rigorous internal logic. The more fantastical the world, the tighter the structure must be.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with one detail that tells the audience this world is different
- Escalate by revealing the human cost of the speculative element
- Turn by showing that the "different" world mirrors our own

**What works on TikTok:** Near-future scenarios that feel one step away from reality. Technology-gone-wrong stories. The twist reveals the human problem beneath the sci-fi premise.

**Hook patterns:**
- The new normal ("In 2847, they finally cured death. That's when the real problems started.")
- The glitch ("My AI assistant started answering questions I hadn't asked yet.")
- The cost ("Time travel costs $4.99 per minute. I had $47 in my account. Enough to say goodbye.")

---

## True Crime / Crime

**Core value:** Justice → Injustice / Truth → Deception
**Convention:** A crime must happen early. POV determines the subgenre.

**Short-form pattern:**
- Hook with the crime or its aftermath
- Escalate by revealing layers of deception or unexpected connections
- Turn by exposing who really did it, or why

**POV subgenres for short-form:**
- **Victim's POV (Thriller):** The audience fears alongside the narrator
- **Detective's POV (Mystery):** The audience solves alongside the narrator
- **Criminal's POV (Caper):** The audience roots for the plan to work
- **Bystander's POV:** The audience sees what no one else does

**What works on TikTok:** Stories that feel like they could be real. The "this actually happened" framing. Details that are too specific to be invented. The turn that makes the audience go back and re-read from the beginning.

**Hook patterns:**
- The cold open ("The 911 call came from inside the house. But no one had lived there for six years.")
- The wrong suspect ("Everyone thought the husband did it. The security footage told a different story.")
- The detail that cracks it ("She said she hadn't seen him in weeks. Her phone said otherwise.")

---

## General Genre Principles for Short-Form

1. **Know your genre's conventions before you break them.** The audience enters with expectations shaped by every story they have ever consumed in your genre.
2. **Keep convention, kill cliche.** Convention is the skeleton. Cliche is a lazy costume. Fulfill the expectation in a way they have never seen.
3. **Mix genres to create surprise.** A romance that turns into horror. A comedy that lands on genuine emotion. Genre-mixing is the fastest path to originality in short-form.
4. **The shorter the story, the purer the genre.** In 60 seconds you have room for one genre, maybe two. Pick your primary genre and commit. Dilution kills impact.
5. **Genre conventions are creative limitations, not restrictions.** They force your imagination to find fresh solutions within known structures. The constraint is the creativity.
