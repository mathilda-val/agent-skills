# Based Design — Surface Techniques

Techniques for color, image treatment, visual depth, texture, layering, z-axis effects, found materials, and visual surface. Consult this file when working with the material and tonal qualities of a page — how layers interact, how color and value signal structure, how photographic and typographic surfaces collide, and how visual depth is constructed on a flat plane.

---

### TECHNIQUE: FIGURE-GROUND REVERSAL AS SECTION MARKER
Inverting the dominant value relationship (dark-on-light → light-on-dark) signals a major tonal shift without conventional dividers. It's the most powerful section-break tool available. Figure-ground inversion creates a distinct hierarchical level independent of scale. A reversed block of at least 15% page area resets reader attention — a structural caesura. *(Issues 05, 08, 19, 23, 25)*

### TECHNIQUE: REVERSED-OUT TYPE BLOCKS AS VISUAL RIVETS
Dark rectangles with reversed-out text function as visual weight anchors and navigational markers. Regardless of size, they register as the densest point on the page — structural fastening points that pin composition. *(Issues 06, 07, 24)*

### TECHNIQUE: LAYERING DEPTH = COGNITIVE DEMAND
Transparency and overlap create implied depth. When 3+ layers coexist, the viewer must choose a focal plane, making reading active rather than passive. The number of layers directly correlates to cognitive demand. *(Issue 10)*

### TECHNIQUE: SOLID BLACK RECTANGLE AS FULL STOP
A solid black rectangle carrying no content functions as a visual anchor and compositional period mark — pure weight that signals the end of a section. A block occupying 20-40% of page area with reversed text creates a structural pause that resets attention. *(Issues 07, 23)*

### TECHNIQUE: BITMAP/PIXEL AS TECHNOLOGICAL SIGNAL
Deliberately low-resolution elements in a high-resolution context create friction — they attract attention because they violate the medium's expected fidelity. Pixel typefaces function as texture generators — they fill space with a gridded pattern that happens to also be readable. *(Issues 10, 12, 15)*

### TECHNIQUE: OBJECT ROTATION AS DEFAMILIARIZATION
Rotating or mirroring typography forces the viewer to engage with letterforms as shapes rather than carriers of meaning. The inverted text is treated as an image element and assigned image-level hierarchy. Presenting text in mirrored/reversed state triggers the reading impulse without satisfying it — 30-40% readable creates productive friction. *(Issues 20, 23, 24)*

### TECHNIQUE: ORNAMENTAL BORDERS AS ARCHITECTURAL ELEMENTS
Repeating border patterns (diamonds, dots, chevrons) at 1-2% of page height, running along page edges or between content sections, function as architectural boundary markers — not decoration. They define content zones without using rules or boxes. The pattern must remain identical in every instance and appear on >50% of editorial pages to establish systemic coherence. *(Issue 31)*

### TECHNIQUE: FORMAT SIMULATION AS CONTENT-FORM UNITY
When content references a specific communication format (email, bureaucratic form, lecture poster), simulate that format's visual conventions rather than translating into the publication's house style. Monospaced type in email header format for email-based content. Institutional type for form-based content. The simulated format adds a meaning layer that paraphrasing into house style would destroy. Must be visually distinguishable from surrounding editorial type at first glance. *(Issues 31, 34)*

### TECHNIQUE: BINARY FIELD COLLISION
When a spread is divided into two zones of maximum contrast (solid black vs. white, image-saturated vs. empty), the spine becomes a structural element, not a neutral transition. The dark/filled field always dominates perceptually. To achieve perceived 50:50 balance, the lighter zone must occupy approximately 60-65% of the spread area. The perceived ratio of a physical 50:50 split is approximately 30:70 in visual weight. *(Issue 35)*

### TECHNIQUE: PHOTOGRAPHIC GROUND REDISTRIBUTES HIERARCHY
When text is overlaid on a full-bleed photograph, all type hierarchy levels are compressed because every element competes equally with the image ground for legibility. Elements that would have 15:1 scale contrast on white paper achieve only ~5:1 perceived contrast on image ground. To maintain readable hierarchy over an image, scale ratios must increase 2-3x compared to the same hierarchy on a neutral ground. *(Issue 35)*

### TECHNIQUE: INVERTED SIZE/VISIBILITY PRINCIPLE
Physical size and perceptual prominence can be decoupled. Print the larger element at 5-15% contrast from the background (ghost type) and the smaller element at 90-100% contrast. The reader sees the small element first and discovers the large element second — temporal hierarchy (what is seen first) contradicts spatial hierarchy (what is biggest). Use sparingly — once per project, as it only works when the reader expects size = prominence. *(Issue 30)*

### TECHNIQUE: PALIMPSEST DEPTH FIELD
Stack photographic, typographic, diagrammatic, and illustrative layers at varying opacities to create a genuine z-axis illusion on a flat page. Text is embedded within specific depth layers rather than floating on top. Lighter layers reveal darker ones beneath; text legibility is selectively controlled by position in the depth stack. This goes beyond standard collage — the layers interact optically, transforming reading into archaeological excavation. Minimum three opacity levels for convincing depth. *(Issues 37, 38)*

### TECHNIQUE: VERNACULAR PASTICHE AS STRUCTURAL RESET
Deploy vernacular design vocabulary (classified ads, hot rod graphics, mail-order catalogs, retro advertising) as structural palate cleansers between refined typographic sections. The vernacular provides a complete alternative grid system (horror vacui density, multi-scale type hierarchy, coupon cut-lines as structural rules) that resets the reader's density expectations through maximum contrast. The key: the vernacular is not decoration — it is a fully operational alternative compositional system. *(Issue 38)*

### TECHNIQUE: COLLAGE-OVER-GRID FRICTION FIELD
Two organizational systems (text grid + photographic collage) occupying the same spatial footprint simultaneously, with neither subordinate. The text grid continues behind collage elements, visible in gaps. This creates perceptual oscillation — the eye processes grid structure in one moment and collage gesture in the next. The friction between systems prevents either from resolving into stable composition. *(Issue 41)*

### TECHNIQUE: CROSS-SPREAD GROUND-TONE COLLISION
Two incompatible tonal environments (dark dense collage on one page, light airy field on the facing page) meeting at the gutter with no transition. The visual dissonance at the gutter is physically felt when the spread is open — a boundary between worlds rather than a structural joint. Generates tension neither page could create alone. *(Issue 43)*

### TECHNIQUE: TRANSPARENT GRID AS SUBLIMINAL STRUCTURE
Content printed at 5-10% contrast from the page surface (ghost characters, faint pattern grids) creates a compositional element below the threshold of conscious perception. The page feels different from blank without the reader articulating why — subliminal ground treatment modifying spatial experience without demanding attention. *(Issue 43)*

### TECHNIQUE: COLOR-CODED VOICE DIFFERENTIATION
Background color tinting at the paragraph level to differentiate speakers in dense multi-column text — green blocks mark one voice, white marks another. Preserves visual density and reading continuity while adding a "who is speaking" signal in a separate perceptual channel (color) from the text channel (form/position). *(Issue 45)*

### TECHNIQUE: FULL-SPREAD PATTERN FIELD AS ANTI-LAYOUT
Content (typographic patterns, ornamental specimens) becoming the layout's structural material — filling an entire spread as uninterrupted texture. The specimens become ground; the product label becomes figure. Content generates its own layout structure rather than being placed within a pre-existing one. *(Issue 44)*

### TECHNIQUE: IMAGE-LAYER TYPOGRAPHY AS PARALLEL TEXT
Reproduced spreads containing display typography that remains legible at reduced reproduction size, operating at effective scale roughly equivalent to editorial body text. Two parallel text streams compete for reading attention — the measured critical voice and the expressive voice of the reproduced work — creating involuntary polyphonic reading. *(Issue 44)*

### TECHNIQUE: PHOTOGRAPHED PERSPECTIVES AS GRID-BREAKING GEOMETRY
Open books photographed at slight angles creating trapezoidal and rhomboid shapes within rectangular page grids. The perspectival distortion is preserved as a compositional element — diagonal energy earned by the object's physicality rather than arbitrary angular elements. Distinguishes from decorative grid-breaking. *(Issue 45)*

### TECHNIQUE: HORIZONTAL IMAGE-FRIEZE AS CROSS-SPREAD BINDING
Small reproductions arranged in a continuous horizontal band across the top of both pages, creating a frieze spanning the gutter. Text below operates independently on each page. Unity through rhythmic repetition of similar-but-distinct elements — structurally closer to an architectural frieze than a typographic device. *(Issue 45)*

### TECHNIQUE: FOLIO NUMBERS AS CHROMATIC PULSE
Page numbers set at 6x body text in a spot color (red), making them disproportionately large for navigation. They create consistent weight points at bottom outer corners and a rhythmic color pulse visible when fanning pages — connecting visually across the issue as a structural heartbeat. *(Issue 44)*
